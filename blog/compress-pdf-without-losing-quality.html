Why PDFs get too large

Most bulky PDFs contain high-resolution images (scanned pages, photos, diagrams) or have embedded fonts and metadata. A good compressor balances three knobs:

  • DPI (render resolution) for pages
  • JPEG quality for rasterized images
  • Cleanup such as metadata removal

Quick way (balanced quality)

  1. Open DocPDFHub — Compress PDF.
  2. Drop your PDF and choose Balanced.
  3. Keep Downsample images and Remove metadata checked.
  4. Click Add PDF to Compress → download the optimized file.

This runs fully in your browser—no upload, private by design.

Best settings for quality

  • Text-heavy PDFs: 110–150 DPI, JPEG quality 75–85.
  • Scans & photos: 150 DPI, JPEG 80–90 to keep edges clean.
  • Vector diagrams: Prefer original vectors when possible. If rasterized, use 150 DPI.

In our tool, the presets map roughly to: Light (150 DPI @ 90), Balanced (110 DPI @ 75), Strong (90 DPI @ 60).

Advanced tips

  • Remove unused pages first with Split PDF.
  • Merge multiple documents after compression with Merge PDF.
  • Convert high-resolution scans to JPEGs once using PDF → JPG, then recreate a lighter PDF if needed.

Troubleshooting

Text looks fuzzy? Increase DPI to 150 or move from Strong → Balanced.

File still too big? Use Strong, or remove images that aren’t needed.

Fonts look different? Some PDFs flatten fonts during optimization; try keeping metadata if your workflow depends on font info.

FAQ

Is this really private? Yes—DocPDFHub processes files locally in your browser.

Does compression change text? Text stays the same; rasterized pages balance quality vs size.

What about scanned PDFs? Use 150 DPI and higher JPEG quality for best legibility.
